## Resumen

Spinal epidural steroid injections (SESI) are commonly used for radiating low back and cervical pain but remain limited by target imprecision, operator variability, and inconsistent outcomes. Artificial intelligence (AI) has&#x202f;been increasingly&#x202f;investigated&#x202f;as a tool to augment image interpretation, trajectory planning,&#x202f;and decision support in&#x202f;interventional&#x202f;procedures, prompting&#x202f;interest&#x202f;in&#x202f;its potential role in procedural guidance for SESI. This scoping review aimed to summarize the current evidence on the use of AI to support procedural guidance for SESI. A search was conducted across three databases,&#xa0;PubMed (National Library of Medicine (NLM)), Web of Science, and Embase,&#xa0;for English-language primary studies published between 2015 and 2025.&#xa0;Nine studies met the inclusion criteria. The AI architectures included machine learning, deep learning, and neural network-based models. Evidence was clustered into three domains: pre-procedural planning&#x202f; (e.g.,&#x202f;anatomic&#x202f;segmentation and&#x202f;trajectory&#x202f;optimization&#x202f;using CT or MRI),&#x202f;intraprocedural guidance&#x202f;(e.g.,&#x202f;real-time needle localization or plane&#x202f;identification using&#x202f;ultrasound&#x202f;or robotic systems), and post-procedural outcome prediction&#x202f;(e.g.,&#x202f;forecasting&#x202f;pain reduction following SESI based on&#x202f;imaging data). Most studies&#x202f;demonstrated&#x202f;improvements in&#x202f;trajectory planning, needle localization, segmentation efficiency, or predictive performance. Potential benefits included reduced puncture time, decreased radiation exposure, and improved patient selection. However, AI performance was model-dependent, with some systems showing higher error rates than others. Additional&#x202f;limitations included small datasets, heterogeneity in AI model design, and underrepresentation of patients with spinal pathology or&#x202f;elevated body mass index. Overall, AI shows promise for enhancing SESI procedural guidance, but larger, more standardized studies are needed before its routine clinical use.
