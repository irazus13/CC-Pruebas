## Resumen

This study develops a fractional-order mathematical model for Hepatitis C Virus (HCV) transmission using a six-compartment framework and the Caputo&#x2013;Fabrizio derivative. The operator&#x2019;s exponential kernel captures essential short-term memory effects in acute infection and treatment response, a key advancement over classical integer-order models. We establish the model&#x2019;s well-posedness through rigorous positivity, boundedness, existence-uniqueness, and stability analyses. The basic reproduction number [Formula: see text] confirms epidemic potential, with sensitivity analysis identifying acute transmission (&#x3b2;2) as the dominant driver, accounting for over 51% of new infections&#x2014;a critical public health insight. Fractional-order simulations demonstrate that increased memory effects (lower fractional order) delay and flatten outbreak waves, underscoring the importance of sustained interventions. A novel Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) is implemented to solve the coupled fractional system. The PINN achieves high predictive accuracy ([Formula: see text], low RMSE) for Susceptible, Exposed, Acute, and Quit Treatment compartments, though performance is moderate for the Chronic stage ([Formula: see text], reflecting its more complex, heterogeneous dynamics. The PINN&#x2019;s success offers a mesh-free, computationally efficient alternative to traditional numerical schemes for complex epidemiological systems. This work&#x2019;s primary contribution is the first integration of a Caputo&#x2013;Fabrizio HCV model with a PINN solver, creating a hybrid mechanistic-data-driven framework. The results provide actionable strategies: prioritizing acute-phase control and implementing long-term, memory-aware public health measures. The model serves as a validated tool for optimizing HCV elimination strategies, particularly in resource-limited settings, while highlighting directions for future refinement, including integration with real-world clinical data.
