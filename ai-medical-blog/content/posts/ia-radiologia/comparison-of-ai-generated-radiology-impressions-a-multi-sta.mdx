## Resumen

A retrospective, blinded evaluation of 200 oncologic computed tomography reports compared original radiologist-authored impressions, impressions generated by a custom domain-specific AI model fine-tuned on institutional data, and impressions generated by a general-purpose large language model. Ten clinicians, including original radiologists (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;4), independent radiologists (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;3), and oncologists (n&#x2009;=&#x2009;3), rated impressions for completeness, correctness, conciseness, clarity, clinical utility, and patient harm. Original and independent radiologists assigned lower preference to generic model impressions (Cohen's h 1.04-1.22 and 0.66-0.69, p&#x2009;&lt;&#x2009;0.001). Original radiologists slightly preferred their own impressions to the custom model (h&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.18, p&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.0716), while independent radiologists showed no preference (h&#x2009;=&#x2009;-0.03, p&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.78). Oncologists demonstrated no significant preference among impression types (h&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.04-0.12, all p&#x2009;&gt;&#x2009;0.20). Custom model impressions achieved near parity with human impressions; original radiologists rated their own impressions slightly more complete (r&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.22, p&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.0016). Generic model impressions were longer (75.1&#x2009;&#xb1;&#x2009;20.4 words), slightly more complete (r&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.18-0.39, p&#x2009;&lt;&#x2009;0.001-0.01), but significantly less concise (r&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.85-0.87, p&#x2009;&lt;&#x2009;0.001). Patient harm ratings were uniformly low (likelihood 1.01-1.14; extent 1.05-1.21). Inter-rater reliability ranged from -0.09 to 0.67 (&#x3b1;&#x2009;=&#x2009;0.67 conciseness; &#x3b1;&#x2009;=&#x2009;-0.09-0.03 clinical utility/correctness).
