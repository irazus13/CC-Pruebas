## Resumen

**OBJECTIVES:** Diagnostic limitations contribute to variability in clinical decision-making, highlighting the need for objective diagnostic tools. Biomarker-based approaches enable detection of subclinical pathological changes and may improve diagnostic accuracy. Exhaled breath contains volatile organic compounds (VOCs) that reflect host and microbial metabolic activity and therefore represent a promising non-invasive diagnostic medium. This study evaluated the feasibility of a nanosensor array outputs to classify caries, gingivitis, periodontitis, and peri-implantitis using breath VOC profiles. **METHODS:** A priori power analysis (&#x3b7;&#xb2;=0.02) yielded 346 participants across five oral phenotypes. Exhaled VOCs were analyzed via GC-MS, with a subset assessed using a 40-sensor nanoarray. VOCs were age-adjusted (residual regression), filtered (&#x2265;50% presence; 47 compounds), log-transformed, and tested (Kruskal-Wallis, Bonferroni &#x3b1;adj=0.0167). Nanoarray data underwent Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (15 factors; &#x3b1;adj=0.01), Canonical Discriminant Analysis (CDA), and supervised Machine Learning (ML) (80:20 split). **RESULTS:** GC-MS identified one significant VOC (RT&#x2248;2.81 min) differentiating periodontitis from caries (p=0.006) and gingivitis (p=0.043), while 13 VOCs distinguished implant groups (p&lt;0.05). Nanoarray CDA showed two significant functions (Wilks' &#x39b;=0.1697, p&lt;0.0001; 87.64% variance). A 10-sensor ML model achieved 69.6% training and 58.8% validation accuracy. Validation sensitivities were polarized (100% periodontitis/healthy implants; 25-50% others), with high specificities (0.714-1.000) and ROC-AUC (0.88-0.97). **CONCLUSIONS:** Although validation performance across all disease categories remained variable, and discriminatory capacity was limited for early dysbiotic and non-inflammatory conditions, the predictive models demonstrated strong classification accuracy for inflammatory phenotypes, particularly periodontitis and implant-related states. These findings highlight both the promise of volatilomic diagnostics and the current limitations imposed by sample size, biological variability, and metabolic overlap, particularly in early disease stages. **CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCE:** Nevertheless, the strong performance observed in inflammatory conditions underscores the potential clinical utility of this platform as a non-invasive adjunct for disease detection and monitoring. Continued advancements in sensor technologies and machine learning methodologies are likely to enhance model robustness and generalizability, facilitating translation into clinical practice.
