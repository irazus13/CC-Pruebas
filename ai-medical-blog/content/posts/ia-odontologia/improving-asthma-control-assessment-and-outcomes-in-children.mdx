## Resumen

**BACKGROUND:** Achieving good asthma control is a goal of asthma management. In children, asthma control and quality-of-life assessments include determining the presence of wheeze. However, wheeze is unreliably reported with high disagreement (&gt;50%) between parental and physician detection of wheeze. Objectively defining wheeze using WheezeScan&#x2122; (a user-friendly, artificial intelligence-based device) could improve assessment of asthma control and hence management. **OBJECTIVE:** Our primary aim is to determine whether adding WheezeScan&#x2122; to routine clinical care improves parental asthma control assessment (ACA) in children (aged 4-11&#x2005;years). Our secondary aims are to examine the impact of WheezeScan&#x2122; upon patient-reported outcomes (PROs), the parent asthma management self-efficacy scale (PAMS), healthcare costs and ease of WheezeScan&#x2122; use. The primary hypothesis is that using WheezeScan&#x2122; alters the ACA group. **METHODS:** Our multicentre prospective cohort study involves 125 children with specialist-confirmed asthma. Over 5&#x2005;weeks, parents/caregivers use the WheezeScan&#x2122; twice-daily at home and whenever wheezing is suspected. After the first week of using the WheezeScan&#x2122;, asthma medications may be adjusted based upon the child's asthma control score and WheezeScan&#x2122; data. Study outcomes are collected at baseline, week&#x2005;1 and week&#x2005;5. Our primary end-point is the proportion of children whose assessment of asthma control changed between week&#x2005;1 and baseline, based upon parental assessments using WheezeScan&#x2122; data. Secondary outcomes are PROs (asthma-related quality of life), PAMS, healthcare costs and WheezeScan&#x2122; ease of use. **CONCLUSIONS:** This protocol describes our study to determine whether using digital technology to accurately identify wheeze in children with asthma improves their asthma assessment and asthma-related PROs, including PAMS and healthcare costs.
